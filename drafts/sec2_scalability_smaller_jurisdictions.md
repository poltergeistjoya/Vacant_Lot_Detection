# 2.x The Scalability Gap: NYC vs. Smaller Jurisdictions

New York City represents a best-case scenario for administrative land use data. With approximately 120 assessors operating within a 2,000-person Department of Finance, NYC is able to maintain a comprehensive, continuously updated parcel database, conduct drive-by photography of every lot twice annually, and target a citywide in-person inspection cycle of three years. The resulting MapPLUTO dataset is a model of municipal data infrastructure: 856,998 parcels with regularly updated building classifications, released publicly on a biannual schedule. And yet, even in this well-resourced context, the classification process is described by practitioners as "pretty manual" and would benefit from automation, or at minimum "some process that would produce a shortlist of parcels to review" (L. McGeehan, NYC Department of Finance, personal communication, April 2026).

Most U.S. municipalities operate with far fewer resources. Smaller jurisdictions typically cannot afford dedicated assessor staff and instead contract third-party valuation companies (such as Vision Government Solutions or Tyler Technologies) to collect parcel data and determine tax roll values. These vendors do not employ remote sensing technology, and their field inspection cycles are typically every three to six years rather than annually. In many cases, parcel boundary data is proprietary and available only for a fee, if at all -- a situation that affects predominantly pre-automobile cities in the Northeast and Rust Belt where land records are often fragmented across county, municipal, and township jurisdictions. One illustrative example: a 10,000-parcel municipality near the Tappan Zee Bridge is managed by just four assessors, a ratio that makes any systematic inspection approach for vacancy detection infeasible without external tools (McGeehan, personal communication).

This capacity gap makes the motivation for automated vacancy detection strongest precisely where data infrastructure is weakest. Cities in the Northeast and Mid-Atlantic -- historically dense, walkable, and underbuilt relative to housing demand -- are also the cities most likely to benefit from infill development on vacant land, and least likely to have the assessor resources to systematically identify it. A model that can generate vacancy shortlists from freely available NAIP imagery without requiring local parcel data could function as a practical planning tool in exactly these contexts.

It is worth noting the distinction between what NYC's data captures and what a deployed model would approximate. NYC's administrative definition of vacancy reflects a legal-fiscal status maintained through human inspection and permit tracking. A remote sensing model cannot replicate this: it cannot read a demolition permit, observe whether a shed is of "nominal value," or know that a foundation was poured last Tuesday. What it can do is flag parcels that exhibit the visual characteristics commonly associated with vacancy -- bare soil, sparse vegetation, absence of rooftop structure -- for human follow-up. For NYC, this is a prioritization aid for assessors who are already doing the work. For a four-assessor municipality covering 10,000 parcels, it may be the primary method of identifying which parcels warrant a site visit at all.
